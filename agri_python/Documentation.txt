1. Introduction 
Agriculture in Nepal is still largely guided by traditional practices, where farmers rely on 
experience and local knowledge to make decisions about what crops to plant, how much 
fertilizer to apply, and when to harvest. While this approach has been passed down through 
generations, it often leads to unpredictable outcomes such as poor yields, crop failures, and 
financial instability. Rapidly changing weather conditions, limited access to scientific 
guidance, and fluctuating market prices make farming even more uncertain, leaving many 
farmers vulnerable to risk. 
To address these challenges, AgriNova has been developed as a mobile-based digital 
advisory system that helps farmers make informed, data-driven decisions. The system is 
designed to be simple and farmer-friendly, ensuring that even those with limited technical 
knowledge can benefit from it. Farmers can enter details about their soil, such as pH levels, 
nutrient content (NPK), rainfall, and district. Based on this input, AgriNova generates 
personalized recommendations for suitable crops and stores the results for future reference. 
The platform also provides basic information about crop requirements, allowing farmers to 
plan their cultivation practices more efficiently. 
The strength of AgriNova lies in its use of the Decision Tree algorithm, which analyzes 
soil conditions and rainfall data to recommend the most suitable crops for cultivation. The 
algorithm evaluates parameters such as soil pH, nitrogen, phosphorus, potassium, and 
rainfall to classify the input data and predict the best crop option. This scientific approach 
ensures that farmers make decisions based on objective data rather than guesswork or 
tradition. Additionally, the system incorporates a Nearest Market Finder module, which 
helps farmers identify and locate the closest agricultural markets to sell their produce 
efficiently. By combining intelligent crop recommendation with accessible market 
information in a single mobile platform, AgriNova empowers farmers with reliable 
guidance, reduces risks, and promotes more productive and sustainable farming practices 
across Nepal across Nepal.

Problem Statement 
Managing agriculture in Nepal through traditional practices presents several challenges for 
farmers. Crop selection is often based on guesswork or local tradition rather than scientific 
data, which can lead to poor yield or crop failure. Fertilizer application is frequently 
imbalanced, either excessive or insufficient, resulting in soil degradation and reduced 
productivity. Farmers also struggle to plan farming activities due to unpredictable weather, 
relying on outdated information or informal sources that may not be reliable. 
Farmers often lack access to proper agricultural guidance and modern decision-support 
systems that could help them choose suitable crops based on their specific soil and 
environmental conditions. As a result, farming outcomes remain uncertain, and the 
potential for higher productivity is not fully realized. Additionally, the absence of a 
centralized system to store and analyze soil data limits the ability to make consistent, data
driven decisions in future farming cycles. 
These challenges highlight the urgent need for a systematic, digital, and data-driven 
advisory tool that can assist farmers with crop planning and selection. Such a system, like 
AgriNova, can use soil parameters such as pH level, nitrogen, phosphorus, potassium, and 
rainfall to recommend the most suitable crop for cultivation. By providing scientific and 
data-based insights, this solution will help improve agricultural productivity, enhance 
sustainability, and empower farmers to make informed decisions that reduce risks and 
improve yields.

Literature Review 
Decision tree methods have been widely adopted for classification tasks in many domains 
due to their interpretability and ease of implementation. Quinlanâ€™s seminal work on 
induction of decision trees established practical algorithms for generating trees from 
labeled data, enabling clear rule-based decisions and fast inference [1]. For AgriNova, 
Decision Trees provide a transparent way to recommend crops: trained on soil and climatic 
attributes, a tree can map combinations of pH, NPK levels, rainfall and season into 
actionable crop choices that farmers can understand and trust.

Clustering techniques, in particular the K-Means algorithm introduced by MacQueen, are 
foundational for grouping similar multivariate observations efficiently [2]. K-Means is well 
suited to partition soil nutrient profiles (N, P, K) into clusters indicating types of nutrient 
deficiency or balance. In AgriNova, applying K-Means to soil test results enables the 
system to recommend fertilizer types and dosages by matching a farmerâ€™s soil profile to the 
nearest cluster prototype and its associated corrective action. 
Regression analysis offers straightforward and statistically grounded methods for 
forecasting continuous variables. Draper and Smithâ€™s presentation of applied regression 
techniques highlights model fitting, diagnostics, and predictionâ€”practical tools for short
term forecasting tasks [3]. For weather forecasting in AgriNova, linear regression (or 
multivariate linear models) can provide efficient, interpretable short-horizon estimates of 
temperature or rainfall when trained on local historical observations and recent API
provided data. 
Time-series smoothing and forecasting methods, such as the moving average and the Box
Jenkins family of models, are effective for detecting trends and short-term patterns in noisy 
data [4]. The moving average technique can smooth daily market prices to reveal the 
underlying trend and reduce volatility, which is exactly what AgriNova requires to present 
farmers with clearer short-term price expectations and to signal likely price rises or falls. 
Graph algorithms are central to routing and network problems. Dijkstraâ€™s shortest path 
algorithm remains a standard, provably optimal method to compute minimum-cost routes 
on weighted graphs [5]. In AgriNova, modeling roads and market locations as a weighted 
graph (weights = distance/time/cost) allows Dijkstraâ€™s algorithm to compute the most 
efficient route from a farmerâ€™s village to the nearest or best market, improving logistics and 
reducing transport costs. 
Instance-based classification, exemplified by the k-nearest neighbors (KNN) rule described 
by Cover and Hart, is useful when labeled examples exist and model interpretability is 
important [6]. KNNâ€™s simplicity and effectiveness for image-based tasks make it a 
reasonable choice for an initial pest/disease detection module in AgriNova: by extracting 
features from leaf images and comparing them to a labeled dataset, the system can classify 
likely diseases and suggest treatments, with the option to replace or augment KNN with 
more advanced CNN models later.

b) Feasibility Study 
i. 
Technical Feasibility 
The proposed system will utilize Flutter for developing a cross-platform mobile application 
that runs smoothly. The backend services will be developed using Python (Django 
framework), which will handle user authentication, data processing, communication with 
the database, and execution of the Decision Tree algorithm. The MySQL database will be 
used as the main data storage system to manage user details, soil inputs, crop 
recommendations, and market data due to its high reliability, scalability, and ease of 
integration with Django. 
Since Flutter, Django, and MySQL are all open-source, stable, and well-documented 
technologies, the development and deployment of the AgriNova system are technically 
feasible. These technologies ensure smooth integration, easy maintenance, and low 
development cost, making the project suitable for an individual student implementation 
while maintaining strong performance and professional functionality. 
ii. 
Operational Feasibility 
The system is designed to be simple and user-friendly, allowing both farmers and 
administrators to use the application without technical expertise. The interface will enable 
farmers to easily input soil parameters and receive instant crop recommendations generated 
by the Decision Tree algorithm. Additionally, the system will include a Nearest Market 
Finder feature that allows farmers to locate the closest agricultural markets based on their 
district or entered location. Administrators can efficiently manage crop and market datasets 
through built-in CRUD operations. The straightforward design ensures smooth operation, 
easy adoption, and minimal need for training or external support. 
iii. 
Economic Feasibility 
The development of AgriNova remains cost-effective as it uses only open-source 
technologies such as Flutter, Django, and MySQL, which do not require expensive licenses. 
The system can operate on standard mobile devices with minimal server requirements, 
making it affordable to deploy and maintain. Since the project architecture supports 
scalability, new modules such as market data or additional crop information can be added 
in the future at a low cost. This ensures that the system remains sustainable, economical, 
and beneficial for long-term use in the agricultural sector.
